the grill

a pellet grill at its core is a very basic device. it has a controller, draft fan, hopper, pellet delivery system, an optional hot rod fire starter, a burn box, heat sheilds, drip tray and the grill. the exhaust and lid are a given.

the first treager grills did not use a hot rod starter system, the had a quick access hatch for you to torch start the fire pot with a handfull of pellets, while rare, this method can still work as a back up if your hot rod fails or shorts out and tripps the GFCI outlet.

three types of controllers, fixed run time, internal temp reactive only, and the programmable dynamic type.

the grill will accumulate ash in the bottom of the grill and the firepot will collect an amount of ash as well, you should periodically vacuum out the ash from grill. i have a dedicated small wet dry vac for the grill. DO OT under any circumstances vacuum a hot grill. i cought a grill filter on fire doing this when i had a grill fire go out. i recovered the cook but nearly lost the vacuum.

always cover your drip tray with aluminum foil. it keeps butn grease from building up and making you fight to scrape it off. please use the heavy duty foil, you will be rewarded if you do.

insulating your grill, there are a few ways to acomplish this. add fire bricks to the empty underside of the grill, use an approved insulation blanket kit, add a second layer of metal to the grill "i believe that treager does this on their timberline series of grills." ........expant this..... conserves pellets, keeps winter out of the grill, watch out for corrosiaon. works like an electric capacitor, probably want to run extended warm up cycles

age is just a number

diff types of aging

drybag
umaidry is the company that makes the dry bags
wet aging
wet aging basicly means keeping it in the bag taht you purchased it in, normally when the cow is butchured the brisket cut gets put immediatly in to the vacuum bag. this limits exposure to microbs.
it is good practice to try to determine the packaging date when you purchase the meat. my experiecne has been that i can routinely buy 14-30 day aged meat at cash-n-carry. I have used 30-45 day aged meat with good results. the first time i tried to wet age a brisket, i aged the meat about 60 days. it was interesting, it was definatly tender, but it had a funny smell (not like rotten), i threw it away but upon further research that was normal and that cut would have cooked up great.
